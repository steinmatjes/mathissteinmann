Developer – Architect Intralogue Pt. 1
May 2026

We usually give architects credit for the built environment. If a building ages well or a neighborhood works, we praise the architect. If something goes wrong, we blame the architect too. That’s fair, for the most part. But it’s only part of the story.

Behind every important building that actually gets built, there’s someone who makes it happen. Someone sees a vision, maybe it’s unusual or costly, and decides it could work, then figures out how. That person is the developer. We rarely talk about them, and when we do, it’s usually as the villain. I studied architecture at ETH and now I’m doing my MSRED at Columbia. Some people were confused by this change. Why leave design for finance? To be honest, I got frustrated. Not with architecture itself, I still think in terms of space and care about how a room feels or how a facade meets the street. I was frustrated by how powerless architecture can be. You can design the best building in the world, but if no one finances, engineers, permits, builds, or leases it, it stays on paper. It’s just a rendering, a competition entry, or an Instagram post. The developer is the one who handles all of that. The quality of the developer shapes the quality of what gets built. This seems obvious, but it still needs to be said because people rarely talk about the built environment this way.

Some people argue that developers are the problem. They say construction is driven by money, and buildings are made to store wealth instead of housing people. Architects are seen as complicit because they give credibility to speculation. The suggested solution is to stop building. I get this argument, and some of it is true. In some European countries, there are more empty homes than the number of homes needed, which makes you wonder what we’re really doing. In Manhattan, you can see residential towers where half the units have never been lived in and ask who those buildings are for. But this argument treats all developers as the same. It’s as if the person who built Ordos, a Chinese ghost city for a million people that no one moved to, and the person who spent twenty years creating a pedestrian street in Cambridge, Massachusetts, are the same. They’re not. The first is a speculator. The second saw that a walkway between two streets would make his building more accessible and improve the neighborhood. That’s a design decision, even though it was made by a developer.

There’s a line from a real estate textbook that I keep thinking about. The author says the developer most likely to succeed in the design phase is someone who can think “qualitatively, quantitatively, nonlinearly, and visually, all at the same time.” When I read that, I thought: he’s describing an architect, even if he doesn’t realize it.
That’s the gap. Finance people describe the ideal developer in ways that sound like an architect’s education. Architects describe the ideal architect as someone untouched by money. Neither side sees what’s clear from the middle: these are the same skills, or at least they should be. What we all should learn, though, is how to balance two things at once: a site plan and a pro forma, a floor plate and a lease structure, a vision for a place and a spreadsheet that shows if it’s possible. You don’t resolve the tension between these things, you manage it, in every project and every decision.

The criticism from architects, that developers ruin everything, is really about bad developers. These are the ones who use architects just for show, treat design as something to cut when budgets are tight, and see buildings only as financial tools with walls. But some developers see design as what makes the financial side work. Good design is good business, not just as a slogan, but as a real way value is created in real estate. A building that works for its tenants leases faster. One that fits its context keeps its value longer. If nobody wants to tear down a building in thirty years, it was probably designed by someone who cared. The real question is who becomes the developer. If it’s someone without any sense of space or how buildings affect people’s lives, you end up with ghost cities, empty towers, and superficial architecture. If it’s someone trained as an architect who then learned finance, you get something different. It’s not always better, but there’s a chance for something better that wouldn’t exist otherwise.

I don’t have a tidy conclusion. The built environment won’t improve just because architects complain about developers or because developers ignore architects. It might get better if more people who think like architects start making the decisions that architects usually don’t get to make. We have leverage that most designers don’t. The developer controls the program, the budget, the site, and the schedule. That’s where the built environment really takes shape, not at the drawing board, but at the negotiating table, the zoning hearing, and the capital stack meeting.
