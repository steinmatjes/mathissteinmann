Developer – Architect Intralogue Pt. 1
May 2026

Summary: Buildings are for people. Neither architects nor developers have consistently put that first, and closing the gap between the two professions is mostly a matter of common sense.

We give architects credit for the built environment. If a building ages well, we praise the architect. If it fails, we blame the architect. This is only partly right. It leaves out the person who decided the building should exist in the first place.

Behind every building that gets built, someone saw a site, imagined what could go there, figured out how to finance it, get it approved, get it constructed, and get it occupied. That person is the developer. And how that person thinks, about space, about people, about what a building is actually for, shapes the outcome more than any other single factor.

This is not how most people in architecture see it. The developer is usually cast as the villain. The one who cuts the budget, kills the design, treats buildings as financial instruments with walls. Some critics go further. They argue that construction has become a machine for storing wealth, not for housing people, and that architects are complicit because they give credibility to speculation. The evidence isn’t thin. In several European countries, there are more vacant homes than the identified housing shortage. In Manhattan, residential towers stand half-empty, their units never lived in. From some corners, the proposed response is radical: stop building altogether.

Parts of this are true. But the argument treats all developers as interchangeable, as if they all build for the same reasons and with the same disregard for the people who will use what they build. Consider two cases. In Ordos, Inner Mongolia, local officials and coal barons poured over a billion dollars into a city planned for a million residents. It included museums, stadiums, an airport, everything except people. Nobody came. In Cambridge, Massachusetts, a developer named William Poorvu spent twenty years convincing neighboring property owners to create a pedestrian walkway between Brattle Street and Mt. Auburn Street. He had a selfish motive, it made his building more accessible, but he also had a bigger picture in mind. The walkway made the neighborhood work better for the people who used it. That’s a design decision. It was made by a developer.

The difference between these two cases isn’t just scale or geography. It’s whether anyone stopped to ask: who is this for? A city built for no one is not a planning failure. It’s a failure of common sense. A walkway that took twenty years of negotiation is not efficient. But someone was thinking about the people at the other end.

Buildings exist for the people who use them. This sounds so obvious it shouldn’t need saying. But both sides of the profession manage to forget it. Architects, at their worst, design for other architects, “architecture for architects” was a term we used in school, and it wasn’t a compliment. Developers, at their worst, build for spreadsheets. Neither puts the person who will live, work, or walk through the building at the center. When someone does, the results speak for themselves. A building that works for its tenants leases faster. One that fits its context holds its value. If nobody wants to tear it down in thirty years, someone was thinking about the people inside it.

There’s a line from a real estate textbook that I keep coming back to. William Poorvu describes the developer most likely to succeed in the design phase as someone who can think “qualitatively, quantitatively, nonlinearly, and visually, all at the same time.” When I read that, I thought: he’s describing an architect. He just doesn’t know it.

That’s the gap. Finance people describe the ideal developer using skills that sound like an architectural education. Architects describe the ideal architect as someone untouched by money. Neither side sees what seems obvious from the middle: these are overlapping skill sets, and the overlap is where common sense lives. A site plan and a pro forma. A floor plate and a lease structure. A vision for a place and a spreadsheet that tests whether it’s possible. The job is holding both at once, not because it’s theoretically interesting, but because that’s how you build something that actually works for people.

I came to this from architecture. I got frustrated, not with design, but with how little power architects have over what actually gets built. You can design the best building in the world, but if no one finances it, permits it, builds it, and leases it, it stays on paper. A rendering. A competition entry. An Instagram post. The developer handles all of that. So that’s where I went.

The real question is who becomes the developer. If it’s someone with no spatial sense, no instinct for how buildings shape daily life, you get ghost cities and empty towers, buildings for no one. If it’s someone trained to think about space and then learned how money works, there’s a chance for something different. Not guaranteed better. But a possibility that wouldn’t exist otherwise.

The developer controls the program, the budget, the site, the schedule. That’s where the built environment takes shape, not at the drawing board but at the negotiating table, the zoning hearing, the capital stack meeting. The built environment won’t improve because architects complain about developers. It might improve if more people who think about buildings the way architects do start making the decisions that architects don’t get to make. That’s not a radical idea. It’s common sense.
